Why milliseconds matter: execution speed, slippage and your P&L

The spread you see quoted is not the cost you pay. Between the moment you click and the moment your order reaches the book, the market keeps moving — and whatever it moves is yours to keep or to lose. That difference is slippage, and it is a direct, mechanical function of latency.

The anatomy of a click

A market order's life: your browser fires it → it travels to the execution servers → the engine matches it against the best available liquidity → the fill confirms back to your screen. On PRIME DCX that round trip clocks in around 12 milliseconds — roughly twenty-five times faster than a human blink. EUR/USD ticks a handful of times per second in normal conditions; at 12ms, the price you clicked and the price you got are almost always the same price.

Where the money leaks

  • Slippage: at slow brokers, fast markets fill you several pips from your click. A strategy that nets 5 pips per trade dies at 2 pips of average slippage — silently.
  • Requotes: the older sin: “price changed, accept the new one?” By the time you answer, the moment is gone. PRIME DCX routes with zero requotes — the order fills or it doesn't; nobody renegotiates it mid-flight.
  • Rejection loops: retrying a rejected order in a moving market compounds both problems. Deep aggregated liquidity — $2.5B+ daily on this book — keeps rejections rare.
Speed compounds like fees do. One pip of avoidable slippage on one daily lot is ≈ $10 a day, ≈ $2,500 a trading year. Execution quality is not a luxury feature; it's a recurring line item on your P&L.

Measure it yourself

Run twenty trades in the Web Trader and compare each fill price against the quote at click time (your order history shows both). Average the difference. That number — not any marketing claim, ours included — is your personal execution cost, and it should sit at or near zero in calm markets.
