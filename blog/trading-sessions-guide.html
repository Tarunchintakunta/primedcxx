The 24-hour market: a session-by-session field guide

FX never really opens and never really closes — it hands the torch westward around the planet, five days a week. But “always open” doesn't mean “always the same.” Each session has a personality, and matching your strategy to the session you actually trade in is one of the cheapest edges available.

Tokyo — 00:00–06:00 GMT

The quiet builder. Yen and Aussie pairs do their honest work here while EUR and GBP crosses drift in narrow ranges. Range traders like Asia precisely because breakouts are rare; the session's highs and lows often become the levels London later attacks. Spreads on JPY pairs are at their best of the day.

London — 08:00–16:30 GMT

The decision-maker. Roughly a third of global FX volume clears through London, and the first two hours regularly set the day's direction. The classic play: watch how price treats the Asian range at the open — a clean break with follow-through tends to trend; a sharp rejection usually means a trap. This is where breakout strategies earn their keep.

New York — 13:30–20:00 GMT

The confirmer — and the amplifier. US data lands at 13:30 GMT, and for three hours London and New York run simultaneously. That overlap is the deepest liquidity window on Earth: the tightest spreads, the fastest fills, and the moves with the most conviction. If you only have two hours a day to trade majors, these are the two.

Dubai — 06:00–14:00 GMT

The bridge. Gulf desks connect Asia's close to London's open, with gold — a regional specialty — particularly active. XAU/USD traders will find the 06:00–10:00 GMT stretch busier than its reputation.

Crypto never sleeps: BTC/USD and ETH/USD trade 24/7 on PRIME DCX, weekends included. Session logic still echoes there — US hours carry the volume — but the guardrails of an FX calendar don't apply. Size accordingly.

Practical settings

  • Trade the London–NY overlap for the lowest all-in costs on majors.
  • Avoid the 21:00–23:00 GMT rollover hour — books thin out and spreads briefly widen.
  • Mark the Asian high/low every morning; London respects them more often than not.
